# Standard Numerical Reconstructions (with assumptions)

## 1. Transatlantic + Intra-American Arrivals (SlaveVoyages)

- **Figure commonly cited**: ~388,000–400,000 Africans disembarked in mainland North America (legal trade).
- **Post-1808 illegal + intra-American**: commonly estimated 50,000–100,000+ (highly uncertain).
- **Assumptions**:
  - European shipping logs and port records are sufficiently complete after imputation.
  - “Disembarked” counts can be treated as net additions to the U.S. population base.
  - No systematic double-counting or re-export bias large enough to overturn the order of magnitude.

## 2. U.S. Census Slave Schedules 1790–1860

- **1790**: 697,624 enslaved
- **1860**: 3,953,760 enslaved
- **Implied annual growth (1790–1860)**: ≈ 2.5 % if the totals are taken as accurate.
- **Assumptions**:
  - Enumerator visual assessment + owner reports produced counts accurate to within a few percent.
  - Category “slave” / “Negro” is demographically stable across decades (no large-scale reclassification or absorption that would change the growth rate).
  - Under-counting of young children or over-counting for tax/valuation purposes is not large enough to erase the growth differential vs. Caribbean/Brazil.

## 3. Cliometric “Natural Increase” Claim (Fogel & Engerman lineage)

- **Claim**: The U.S. enslaved population exhibited robust natural increase after the 1808 ban because of relatively balanced sex ratios, temperate climate, lower mortality than sugar colonies, and planter incentives for reproduction.
- **Assumptions**:
  - Plantation ledgers and height/anthropometric data from trader manifests are representative of the general enslaved population (not selected for marketability).
  - Micro-level profitability implies demographic sustainability.
  - Absence of continuous large-scale imports is correctly measured by the legal ban + estimated smuggling volumes.

## 4. Comparative Natural Decrease (Caribbean / Brazil)

- Jamaica, Barbados, Brazil, Cuba: documented net natural decrease requiring continuous imports.
- **Assumption**: The same measurement standards (owner reports, colonial censuses) that are trusted for the U.S. growth rate are also trusted for the Caribbean/Brazilian decrease rates.

---

**Zero-weight treatment**: All of the above numerical totals and growth rates become undefined. The comparative *pattern* (U.S. recorded increase vs. Caribbean/Brazilian recorded decrease) survives only as a qualitative observation about the shape of the administrative record, not as a fact about biological demography.
