# WPA Slave Narratives Collection (Federal Writers’ Project)

**Source**: Library of Congress  
https://www.loc.gov/collections/slave-narratives-from-the-federal-writers-project-1936-to-1938/

Full digitized collection + Supplementary Series (state archives) + HBCU-directed collections (Fisk, Southern University).

**Provenance**: Direct interviews with formerly enslaved people, conducted 1936–1938 (mostly) by predominantly white interviewers under Jim Crow conditions, 70+ years after emancipation.  
**Enslaved voice?**: **YES** — the only large systematic collection of formerly-enslaved testimony (~2,300 interviews). Represents roughly 0.02% of the estimated 4 million formerly enslaved population.  
**Reliability note**: Survives zero-weight mode as the primary qualitative voice source. Cannot support precise quantitative demography (fertility rates, exact growth, import totals).

---

**Key facts**:
- ~2,300 interviews in the main LOC collection.
- Additional volumes in Supplementary Series and HBCU projects (e.g., Fisk University “Unwritten History of Slavery”, Virginia Writers’ Project “The Negro in Virginia”).
- Linguistic evidence (Turner, “Africanisms in the Gullah Dialect”) and cultural documentation (“Drums and Shadows”) also preserved.

**Surviving qualitative signals**:
- First-person accounts of family separation, forced labor, living conditions, and resistance exist.
- The extreme scarcity of pre-1865 systematic enslaved testimony is itself structural evidence of erasure (anti-literacy laws + legal non-personhood).
- These narratives remain the largest window into lived experience that the quantitative administrative record lacks.
