# Racial Classification Laws & Paper Ethnocide

**Key primary / near-primary sources**:
- Virginia Racial Integrity Act (1924) and Walter Plecker reclassification campaign records
- South Carolina Negro Act (1740)
- Colonial Virginia statutes and court reclassification cases
- Dunbar (1863) continental comparison (separate Negro vs. Indian categories in Spanish America)
- Dawes Rolls and Federal Indian termination-era records
- Louisiana Code Noir / Spanish Florida records

**Provenance**: Legislative and administrative records of the classifying authorities.  
**Enslaved voice?**: N/A for the statutes themselves; limited court petitions exist from free/enslaved individuals.  
**Reliability note**: The existence and operation of these legal regimes survive zero-weight mode. They demonstrate that racial categories were administrative tools that could erase or consolidate identities on paper.

---

**Surviving qualitative signals**:
- U.S. law developed a binary “Negro / White” system while Spanish America maintained separate categories for Indigenous enslaved persons (Dunbar’s table).
- “One-drop” rules and the Racial Integrity Act forced mixed individuals into a single racial box.
- Enumerator “lookerism” + community reputation + legal-economic incentives shaped who was counted as what.
- These mechanisms produced both the demographic patterns visible in the records and the systematic erasure of more granular ethnic identities (“paper ethnocide”).
- The same administrative apparatus that generated the quantifiable census and ledger data also controlled the categories those data used.
