# African Burial Ground National Monument (New York) & Related Sites

**Sources**:
- NPS African Burial Ground: https://www.nps.gov/afbg/index.htm
- Full GSA/NPS archaeological reports (15,000–20,000 estimated burials)
- Related sites: Catoctin Furnace (Science 2023), Anson Street Charleston (PNAS 2023), Chesapeake Bay (Current Biology 2023), Harlem African Burial Ground (2024–2025 fieldwork), Charleston Black Burial Grounds GIS, Philadelphia, Colonial Williamsburg, etc.

**Provenance**: Archaeological excavation + physical remains + (in later projects) community-engaged aDNA.  
**Enslaved voice?**: PARTIAL — the bodies themselves are physical testimony; descendant communities involved in several modern projects.  
**Reliability note**: Independent of written administrative head-counts. Survives zero-weight mode as primary physical evidence of multi-generational presence on American soil.

---

**Key points**:
- New York African Burial Ground: estimated 15,000–20,000 individuals of African descent buried in the 17th–18th centuries beneath lower Manhattan.
- Multiple 17th–19th century burial grounds across the eastern United States contain individuals whose remains were interred on American soil.
- Some individuals have genetic links to living descendants (e.g., Catoctin Furnace → tens of thousands of living matches via commercial databases).

**Surviving qualitative signals**:
- Multi-generational presence of people of African and mixed descent is physically documented in the ground.
- Classification fluidity and cultural mixing appear in the archaeological record (African, Native American, and European elements co-occurring).
- These sites function as “memory sites and genealogical sources” independent of owner-authored ledgers.
